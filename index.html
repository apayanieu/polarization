Polarized by Design

Two opinions — fake-believers and debunkers — spread through a network of fixed friendships. With every exchange, ties between people who agree grow a little stronger; ties between people who disagree grow a little thinner. No friendship is ever cut. Yet, as you watch, the population separates into chambers, and the bonds across the divide quietly wither toward zero.

The model in one breath

Every node carries one of two opinions — F (forwarder) or D (debunker). At each tick, a node looks at its disagreeing neighbors only. Each one exerts a pull proportional to the strength of their tie \(w_{ij}\), scaled by a global stubbornness \(S\):

\[ p_{\mathrm{flip}}(i,t) = 1- \prod_{j:\,x_j(t)\neq x_i(t)} \left(1-\frac{w_{ij}(t)}{S}\right) \]

Every node draws one uniform coin and flips with that probability. All flips are applied simultaneously. Then, with the new opinions in hand, every friendship is updated: if the two endpoints now agree, the weight grows by a; if they disagree, it shrinks by a. Weights are clipped to [0, 1]. The edges themselves never appear or disappear.

The polarization coefficient is the complement of the average cross-opinion weight:

\[ P(t) = 1-\frac{1}{|E_{\mathrm{cross}}(t)|} \sum_{(i,j)\in E_{\mathrm{cross}}(t)} w_{ij}(t), \qquad E_{\mathrm{cross}}(t)=\{(i,j)\in E:\,x_i(t)\neq x_j(t)\}. \]

It climbs from near zero (the camps are still well-connected) toward one (the bonds across the divide have withered).

What the layout is telling you

The spatial arrangement of nodes is driven by their opinions, not by edge weights. Two nodes that share an opinion are pulled close; two nodes that disagree are pushed apart. This is a deliberate choice: it makes the chambers visible to your eye as they form.

The wiring of the network, however, never changes. Every edge that exists at t = 0 still exists at t = ∞. What you see fading toward invisibility are the weights of cross-camp edges — the ties that span the divide and have grown too thin to carry persuasion across.

If you were to flatten every edge to the same neutral color, you would see exactly the same topology you started with — just rearranged in space. Polarization here is not a graph that broke. It is a graph whose bridges went silent.

Parameter intuition

Low S (≈ 2–8): everyone is easily persuaded. A small minority can be absorbed by the majority before the cross-camp edges erode. The system often collapses to consensus, and P(t) reaches 1 trivially because there are no cross-opinion edges left.

High S (≈ 50–100): everyone is stubborn. Few opinions flip; the camps stay mixed in space and the bridges erode slowly. P(t) climbs but the visual chambers form lazily.

Mid S with moderate a (≈ S = 5–20, a = 0.03–0.08): the interesting regime. Persuasion happens at the edges of clusters, the surviving cross-camp ties decay before they can re-strengthen, and the two chambers separate visibly.

Larger a accelerates everything: the race finishes faster, more often in favor of fragmentation. Larger N makes the final picture cleaner but slower to settle. Watts–Strogatz with its long-range shortcuts gives the most striking chamber-formation; Erdős–Rényi mixes more thoroughly; Barabási–Albert creates hubs that anchor whichever opinion they start with.
